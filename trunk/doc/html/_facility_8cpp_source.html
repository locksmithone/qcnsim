300 /* Note: this function, as it is, is *not* incrementing # of releases upon a successful preemption.
301 * This is in contrast with SMPL approach. Although the current approach will yield a correct
302 * Mean of Busy Time calculation, problems might arise if a preempted token never goes back to service.
303 * If a preempted token never goes back to service, then the service time is has received so far did
304 * go into Sum of Busy Time, but a # release count will never be accumulated for this token. Therefore,
305 * the effect is adding time to the Sum of Busy Time for no specific token, causing an "error" in the
306 * computation of the Mean of Busy Time.
307 *
308 * (more)
309 * This SMPL behavior has effects on the correct calculation of certain statistics,
310 * namely Sum of Busy Time and Mean Busy Period. Mean Busy Period relies on # of releases to calculate the mean.
311 * A preempted token moving back into service, and then released, can be technically considered as two tokens:
312 * one is serviced up to time t and then "released"; the second is serviced for the remaining service time and then
313 * released. Sum of Busy Time accumulates both parts of the service (before preemption, and after resuming service),
314 * and Number of Releases accumulates two releases for the same token. The final Mean Busy Period will be slightly different
315 * than accumulating one whole service time (the sum of the two parts of the service for the preempted token) and dividing by
316 * *one* single release for the same token.
317 * The larger the value of Sum of Busy Time and # of Releases, the less the difference, but it exists.
318 * SMPL treatment also causes problems if # of Releases is used as # of Tokens serviced. A preempted token will be counted twice.
319 * A simple fix is to obtain the # of Serviced tokens as # of Releases - # of Preemptions.
320 * And, perhaps, use, for calculating the Mean Busy Period, the # of Serviced Tokens, and not # of Releases.
